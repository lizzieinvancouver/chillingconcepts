\emph{The search for better indicators}

Increasing evidence suggests that callose and its degradation through 1,3-$\beta$-glucanases may control bud endodormancy \citep{rinne2011,vanderschoot2014,pan2023epigenetic,pandey2026variable}, potentially alongside phytohormones \citep[][]{tylewicz2018photoperiodic,pan2021aba}---but how useful any of these will be as measurable indicators is unclear. One major challenge is that all the compounds found so far are also part of other plant development and defense pathways, thus methods need to disentangle these other processes from bud dormancy and budbreak.  Using specific genetic markers directly associated with regions and tissues within the dormant bud may therefore be a possible way forward. For example, measuring callose deposition and degradation in the bud may be possible through upregulated expression of 1,3-$\beta$-glucanases and related compounds localized to the plasmodesmata during the overwintering/endodormancy period, though this is far from simple. Similarly, phytohormones, such as ABA and GA, have also been repeatedly implicated and have similarly measurable counterparts (ABA levels increase during dormancy induction and decrease during dormancy release), but do not yet provide a clear indicator. 

Transcriptomics-based approaches allow for detection and quantification of candidate genes involved in budbreak via temporal changes and alignments in transcript expression that are specific to the process targeted, rather than attempting to deduce the probability that changing concentrations of callose, ABA, or GA - all ubiquitous and present in other tissues - are directly involved in just one piece of a specific phenological pathway. This resolution is further assisted by visualizing the gene product to observe subcellular localization, as well as finer-scale methods like testing gene function and interaction through experiments with gene mutants.  

Finding a particular transcript or ratio of transcripts would be likely far easier to measure than callose-related compounds, ABA or gibberellin. One possibility may be in some of the dormancy -associated MADS-box genes (DAM), which are important central regulators of bud dormancy in perennial species \citep{falavigna2019want,yang2021bud}. \citet{zhang2023ppmapk6} have recently shown that during endodormancy in peaches, ABA promotes the expression of PpDAM6 while simultaneously inhibiting the expression of an interacting mitogen-activated protein kinase (PpMAPK6). The expression of PpMAPK6 increases as ABA content decreases during the chilling-induced transition from endodormancy to ecodormancy. Phosphorylation of PpDAM6 by PpMAPK6 may then accelerate its degradation and promote dormancy release. ABA also mediates callose deposition and plasmodesmata closure during dormancy development \citep{tylewicz2018photoperiodic}. After prolonged chilling, gibberellic acid (GA) biosynthesis is activated and the callose is degraded \citep{rinne2011,singh2018genetic}.

The same concepts apply to callose and GA both as separate molecules and in their interactions. For example, 1,3-β-glucanases localized to the plasmodesmata have displayed upregulated expression during the overwintering/endodormancy period, measuring callose deposition and degradation in the bud may be possible through this upregulation; this deposition and the closure of plasmodesmata also appear to be mediated by ABA (Tylewicz et al., 2018). Prolonged chilling results in the upregulation of GA biosynthesis, which in turn induces the upregulation of some 1,3-β-glucanases responsible for plasmodesmata reopening (Rinne et al., 2011; Singh et al., 2018). As such, the 1,3-β-glucanase family could be yet another targetable, clear indicator for modelling dormancy progression and budbreak; they respond to a specific substrate (callose), can be visualized both transcriptomically (qPCR) and through reporters to visualize their expression patterns, are localized to specific tissues 
(plasmodesmata), and interact with master regulators like phytohormones as part of a larger system that regulates the timing of budbreak.
