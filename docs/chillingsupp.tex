\emph{The search for better indicators}

Increasing evidence suggests that callose and its degradation through 1,3-$\beta$-glucanases may control bud endodormancy \citep{rinne2011,vanderschoot2014,pan2023epigenetic,pandey2026variable}, potentially alongside phytohormones \citep[such as ABA and GA,][]{tylewicz2018photoperiodic,pan2021aba}---but how useful any of these will be as measurable indicators is unclear. Approaches focused on measuring these compounds directly, for example callose deposition and degradation in the bud, or changing concentrations of ABA and GA, are far from simple and unlikely to prove successful alone. One major challenge is that all the compounds found so far are also part of other plant development and defense pathways, thus methods need to disentangle these other processes from bud dormancy and budbreak. 

Using transcriptomic approaches may be one possible way forward.  These approaches estimate the relative transcript expression levels of genes implicated in the dormancy cycle, which can be used as proxies for callose, ABA, and GA while concurrently providing molecular insights into the underlying mechanisms responsible.
Rather than attempting to deduce the probability that changing concentrations of callose, ABA, or GA (all ubiquitous and present in other tissues), transcriptomics-based approaches can help detect and quantify candidate genes involved in budbreak via temporal changes and alignments in transcript expression that are specific to dormancy and budbreak. These approaches can then be combined with others---such as visualizing the gene product to observe subcellular localization, as well as finer-scale methods like testing gene function and interaction through experiments with gene mutants. 

For example, \citet{zhang2023ppmapk6} used quantitative PCR (one type of transcriptomics), molecular visualization, and ABA treatments to connect several genes to how ABA may control bud dormancy in peaches. Using this suite of approaches they showed that a DAM gene called PpDAM6 \citep[MADS-box dormancy associated genes, which are important central regulators of bud dormancy in perennial species][]{falavigna2019want,yang2021bud} and an associated phosphorylator, called PpMAPK6 (a mitogen-activated protein kinase) are both regulated by ABA. Experiments showed that ABA upregulated PpDAM6 expression and simultaneously inhibited PpMAPK6, sustaining endodormancy. Reduced ABA concentration as endodormancy progresses toward ecodormancy led to the loss of suppression of PpMAPK6, putatively enabling the phosphorylation of PpDAM6 that marks it for degradation and thereby promotes the transition from endodormancy to ecodormancy. \citet{zhang2023ppmapk6} therefore illustrated a combined method of first screening for candidate genes implicated in the dormancy release phenomenon then tested for their interactions with exogenous ABA application. This clarified the roles of three agents within the dormancy cycle. Thus, at least one of these genes could be a useful marker for endodormancy maintenance given its observed relative transcript levels over time and interaction with ABA, and was also used as a measurable, specific proxy for one of ABA’s many roles in this system. % Further screening for these candidate genes whether through transcriptomics or more targeted genetic analyses using dormancy-associated mutant lines shows potential. 

These same concepts apply to callose and GA, both as separate molecules and in their interactions. For example, 1,3-$\beta$-glucanases localized to the plasmodesmata have displayed upregulated expression during the overwintering/endodormancy period, measuring callose deposition and degradation in the bud may be possible through this upregulation; this deposition and the closure of plasmodesmata also appear to be mediated by ABA \citep{tylewicz2018photoperiodic}. Prolonged chilling results in the upregulation of GA biosynthesis, which in turn induces the upregulation of some 1,3-$\beta$-glucanases responsible for plasmodesmata reopening \citep{rinne2011,singh2018genetic}. As such, the 1,3-β-glucanase family could be yet another targetable, clear indicator for modelling dormancy progression and budbreak; they respond to a specific substrate (callose), can be visualized both transcriptomically (qPCR) and through reporters to visualize their expression patterns, are localized to specific tissues

One possibility may be in some of the dormancy -associated MADS-box genes (DAM), which are important central regulators of bud dormancy in perennial species \citep{falavigna2019want,yang2021bud}. \citet{zhang2023ppmapk6} have recently shown that during endodormancy in peaches, ABA promotes the expression of PpDAM6 while simultaneously inhibiting the expression of an interacting mitogen-activated protein kinase (PpMAPK6). The expression of PpMAPK6 increases as ABA content decreases during the chilling-induced transition from endodormancy to ecodormancy. Phosphorylation of PpDAM6 by PpMAPK6 may then accelerate its degradation and promote dormancy release. ABA also mediates callose deposition and plasmodesmata closure during dormancy development \citep{tylewicz2018photoperiodic}. After prolonged chilling, gibberellic acid (GA) biosynthesis is activated and the callose is degraded \citep{rinne2011,singh2018genetic}.
